Pricing & Tax Information

The typical price of a geothermal power plant can vary widely depending on factors such as the plant's capacity, technology used (binary cycle, flash steam, dry steam), location, and specific project requirements. Generally, geothermal power plants cost an average of $5 million (with prices ranging from $3 million to $10 million). However, since these can usually serve up to 3000 households, the average cost per home is only around $1000 to $3400. Considering that these facilities often last for more than 25 years (during which you get free or highly subsidized electrcity), this initial investment pays off long-term for communities.

Federal Tax Credits

A tax credit is a financial incentive provided by the government to encourage specific behaviors, such as adopting renewable energy solutions like geothermal energy. The Production Tax Credit (PTC) allows owners and developers of geothermal facilities (both large-scale and small-scale) to claim a federal income tax credit on every kilowatt-hour of electricity sold to an unrelated party for a period of 10 years after a facility is placed into service. A federal income tax credit for capital investments in renewable energy projects, the Investment Tax Credit (ITC) is a one-time credit of up to 30% based on the dollar amount of the investment and is earned when the equipment is placed into service.
It's important to note that tax credit rates may change, so staying updated on the latest regulations is advisable.

To be eligible for the federal renewable energy tax credit for communities or businesses, you must meet certain criteria:
Recent Construction: Your installation must be new, or built before a certain deadline.
Location: Tax credits vary widely based on location. Certain locations can pay more.

It's essential to understand that tax credits are applied to your federal tax liability, reducing the amount of taxes you owe. If the tax credit exceeds your tax liability, the excess can typically be carried forward to the following year.

To claim the federal geothermal tax credit, communities or businesses will need to complete IRS Forms 8835, 5695 or 3468 when filing annual tax returns. Consider consulting with a tax professional to ensure accurate documentation and to navigate any updates or changes to the tax credit program. These incentives make geothermal energy financially viable and support environmental sustainability.

Frequently Asked Questions

Geothermal resources are typically found in regions with specific geological characteristics. These include:

  • Tectonic Plate Boundaries: Geothermal resources are commonly found near tectonic plate boundaries, where the Earth's crust is thinner. This thin crust allows heat from the mantle to reach the surface more easily.
  • Volcanic Regions: Regions with active volcanoes often have accessible geothermal reservoirs due to the presence of magma beneath the Earth's surface, contributing to elevated temperatures.
  • Hot Springs and Geysers: Surface manifestations like hot springs and geysers indicate the presence of geothermal activity. These areas provide visible signs of subsurface heat.

There are some environmental considerations with geothermal power plants. These are listed below:

  • Subsurface Fluid Management: Geothermal power plants involve the extraction of fluids from the Earth's subsurface. Proper management of these fluids, including reinjection to avoid depletion, is crucial to maintain the sustainability of the resource. Enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) and reinjection practices help sustain the long-term viability of geothermal reservoirs.
  • Induced Seismicity: The injection or extraction of fluids for geothermal operations may induce seismic activity. This is a consideration for minimizing potential risks. Careful reservoir management, monitoring, and adherence to best practices help minimize the potential for induced seismicity.

Geothermal energy proves highly versatile for various industrial applications, offering sustainable and cost-effective solutions. One key application is direct heating, where moderate-temperature geothermal fluids are employed directly for industrial processes such as food drying, greenhouse heating, and district heating. This direct utilization of geothermal heat contributes to energy efficiency and reduces reliance on conventional heating methods. Furthermore, geothermal power plants play a crucial role in providing electricity for industrial facilities. By harnessing the Earth's internal heat, geothermal power generation offers a reliable and continuous source of energy, contributing to the stability of energy supply for energy-intensive industries like manufacturing. Additionally, high-temperature geothermal fluids find application in specific industrial processes, including drying, pasteurization, and sterilization. Industries can tap into the thermal energy extracted from geothermal reservoirs to optimize their operations, enhance efficiency, and reduce environmental impact. The integration of geothermal energy into industrial practices exemplifies its potential to support sustainable development, reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and foster a transition towards cleaner and more resilient industrial processes.

There are three main types of geothermal power plants:

  • Binary Cycle Plants: This type of power plant uses the heat from geothermal fluid to vaporize a secondary fluid with a lower boiling point, such as an organic Rankine cycle fluid. The vaporized fluid then drives a turbine, which generates electricity. These plants are most suitable for lower-temperature geothermal resources, which have increased efficiency with binary cycle technology.
  • Flash Steam Plants: Flash steam plants use high-pressure hot water from the geothermal reservoir. The high-pressure water is allowed to rapidly expand (flash) into steam, which is then used to drive a turbine connected to a generator. They are efficient for moderate to high-temperature resources, and are widely used in geothermal power generation.
  • Dry Steam Plants: Dry steam power plants use high-pressure steam directly from the geothermal reservoir to turn a turbine, generating electricity. This is the oldest and simplest type of geothermal power plant. They directly utlize steam for power generation, and so they have high efficiency.

The depth of geothermal wells is influenced by factors such as the temperature and depth of the geothermal resource. Generally, geothermal wells can range from a few hundred feet to several thousand feet deep. In areas with high-temperature resources, wells may need to reach greater depths to tap into the Earth's internal heat effectively. The drilling process involves penetrating the Earth's subsurface to access the geothermal reservoir, where hot fluids and steam can be harnessed for power generation or direct use. The drilling depth is determined by the specific characteristics of the geothermal resource, and drilling technology advancements continue to improve efficiency and reach greater depths.

Regarding the lifespan of geothermal power plants, they typically last for several decades. The average lifespan ranges from 20 to 50 years, depending on factors such as maintenance practices, technological advancements, and the specific type of geothermal power plant. With proper upkeep and regular maintenance, geothermal power plants can operate efficiently for an extended period. Advances in technology and ongoing research may contribute to further extending the lifespan of geothermal power plants, ensuring a reliable and sustainable source of clean energy over the long term.
