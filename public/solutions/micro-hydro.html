Pricing & Tax Information

Pricing for microhydro dams may vary based on factors such as system capacity, installation requirements, and location. On average, before federal tax credits, it costs around $20,000 to install residential dams on farms and ponds (ranging between $10,000 to $50,000). On the other hand, utility-scale facilities used by communities or businesses can range from $5 million to $10 million. However, since these can usually serve up to 2000 households, the average cost per home is only around $2500 to $5000. Considering that these facilities often last for more than 30 years (during which you get free or highly subsidized electrcity), this initial investment pays off long-term for communities.

Federal Tax Credits

A tax credit is a financial incentive provided by the government to encourage specific behaviors, such as adopting renewable energy solutions like hydropower. The Production Tax Credit (PTC) allows owners and developers of hydropower facilities (both large-scale and small-scale) to claim a federal income tax credit on every kilowatt-hour of electricity sold to an unrelated party for a period of 10 years after a facility is placed into service. A federal income tax credit for capital investments in renewable energy projects, the Investment Tax Credit (ITC) is a one-time credit of up to 30% based on the dollar amount of the investment and is earned when the equipment is placed into service.
It's important to note that tax credit rates may change, so staying updated on the latest regulations is advisable.

To be eligible for the federal renewable energy tax credit for communities or businesses, you must meet certain criteria:
Recent Construction: Your installation must be new, or built before a certain deadline.
Location: Tax credits vary widely based on location. Certain locations can pay more.

To be eligible for the federal renewable energy tax credit for residential turbines, you must meet certain criteria:
Ownership: You must own, not lease, the wind turbine either through purchase or financing.
Residence: The wind turbine must be installed on a residential property.

It's essential to understand that tax credits are applied to your federal tax liability, reducing the amount of taxes you owe. If the tax credit exceeds your tax liability, the excess can typically be carried forward to the following year.

To claim the federal hydropower tax credit,individuals will need to complete IRS Form 5695 and communities or businesses will need to complete IRS Forms 8835 or 3468 when filing annual tax returns. Consider consulting with a tax professional to ensure accurate documentation and to navigate any updates or changes to the tax credit program. Frequently Asked Questions

Microhydro and conventional hydropower dams differ primarily in scale, application and environmental impact.

Microhydro dams are smaller in size, typically designed for local or small-scale electricity generation. They often have capacities ranging from a few kilowatts to a few megawatts. Conventional hydropower dams, on the other hand, are large-scale installations with capacities measured in tens or hundreds of megawatts.

Microhydro systems are often decentralized and designed for local power generation, serving communities, farms, or small industries. Conventional hydropower dams are part of centralized grid systems, providing electricity to larger populations and industries.

Microhydro projects generally have a lower environmental impact than large dams. They often involve run-of-river systems, minimizing disruption to river ecosystems. In contrast, conventional hydropower dams may have more significant environmental consequences, including altered river flow, habitat disruption, and potential fish migration issues.

Microhydro dams, while generally considered environmentally friendly, can have some impacts. Some are listed below.

  • Fish Migration: Dams can pose challenges for fish migration, but fish-friendly turbine designs and fish passage systems help mitigate these impacts.
  • Sedimentation: The trapping of sediment behind a dam can affect downstream river ecosystems. Proper management strategies are employed to address sedimentation concerns.
  • Habitat Alteration: Changes in water flow can impact local habitats. Run-of-river designs aim to minimize alterations, but some impact is inevitable.
  • Water Temperature: Alterations in water flow can influence water temperature, affecting aquatic life. Proper design and operational practices can mitigate these impacts.

The required water flow for a microhydro dam depends on the system's capacity and head (vertical drop). Generally, microhydro systems operate within a range of flow rates, typically from a few gallons per minute (GPM) to several hundred GPM. Higher flow rates may be needed for larger systems.

The design process involves assessing the available head and flow to determine the appropriate turbine type and size. Site-specific characteristics, such as seasonal variations in water flow, are also considered to optimize the system's performance

Microhydro systems require regular maintenance to ensure reliability and efficiency. Clearing debris from intake screens prevents clogging and ensures a consistent water supply to the turbine. Regular checks on the turbine components, such as blades and bearings, are essential for identifying and addressing wear or damage. Inspecting the pipeline for leaks, corrosion, or blockages helps maintain efficient water flow. Checking and maintaining the generator, including electrical components, ensures smooth operation and optimal power generation. Monitoring environmental impacts, such as sedimentation, fish migration, and habitat alterations, allows for adaptive management practices.

Several factors impact the efficiency of a microhydro system. These are listed below:

  • Head and Flow: The head (vertical drop) and flow rate of water are critical factors. Higher heads and flows generally contribute to higher efficiency.
  • Turbine Type: The choice of turbine (e.g., Pelton, Francis, or Kaplan) depends on the site's characteristics. Each turbine type has an optimal range of heads and flows for maximum efficiency.
  • Pipe and Penstock Design: The design of the pipeline (penstock) that carries water to the turbine influences efficiency. Proper sizing, material selection, and minimizing friction losses are essential.
  • Generator Efficiency: The generator's efficiency in converting mechanical energy into electrical energy is crucial. High-quality generators with efficient conversion contribute to overall system efficiency.
  • Maintenance: Regular maintenance, including cleaning of intake screens and turbine components, ensures optimal performance over the system's lifespan.
